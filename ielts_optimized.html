📘 Topic 1: Present Simple — Facts, Routines, General Truths ▼

When & Why to Use Present Simple

The Present Simple describes permanent situations, general truths, habits, routines, scientific facts, and things that are always or generally true. In IELTS, it is the foundational tense for:

  • Writing Task 1: Overview statements — describing what a graph/chart/diagram shows
  • Writing Task 1: Process diagrams — describing steps in a process
  • Writing Task 2: General claims, thesis statements, stating facts and opinions
  • Speaking Part 1: Describing habits, routines, preferences
  • Speaking Part 3: Making general statements about society
  • Reading: Recognizing factual statements and research findings

Structure & Form

Affirmative: Subject + V1 (+ s/es for 3rd person singular)
Negative: Subject + do/does + not + V1
Question: Do/Does + Subject + V1?

Note: Third person singular (he/she/it/the graph/the data) takes -s: work → works, study → studies, go → goes, show → shows

📘 Topic 2: Present Continuous — Temporary Situations, Current Trends ▼

When & Why to Use Present Continuous

The Present Continuous describes actions happening now, temporary situations, current trends, developing changes, and ongoing processes. In IELTS, this tense is essential for:

  • Writing Task 1: Describing current trends that are still developing
  • Writing Task 2: Discussing current changes in society ("is becoming", "are increasing")
  • Speaking Part 1: What you're doing these days / currently
  • Speaking Part 3: Current societal trends
  • Reading: Recognizing temporary vs permanent situations in passages

Structure & Form

Affirmative: Subject + am/is/are + V-ing
Negative: Subject + am/is/are + not + V-ing
Question: Am/Is/Are + Subject + V-ing?

Spelling rules: run → running (double consonant); make → making (drop -e); lie → lying (ie → y)

Signal Words / Time Expressions

📕 Topic 3: Past Simple — Completed Events, Historical Data ▼

When & Why to Use Past Simple

The Past Simple describes completed actions in a finished time period. It is the workhorse tense for IELTS:

  • Writing Task 1: Describing data from completed past periods (e.g., 2000–2015)

Negative: Subject + did + not + V1
Question: Did + Subject + V1?

V2: Regular: -ed (worked, studied); Irregular: went, saw, heard, had, took, made, gave, spoke, wrote, thought

📕 Topic 4: Past Continuous — Background Actions, Narrative Development ▼

When & Why to Use Past Continuous

The Past Continuous describes background actions, interrupted actions, two simultaneous past events, and changing situations in the past. For IELTS:

  • Speaking Part 2: Setting the scene in narratives ("I was walking when…")

  • Writing Task 1: Describing simultaneous trends ("While X was increasing, Y was declining")

Structure & Form

Negative: Subject + was/were + not + V-ing
Question: Was/Were + Subject + V-ing?

Key patterns:
• While + past continuous, + simple past (interruption)
• While + past continuous, + past continuous (simultaneous)
• Subject + was/were + V-ing + when + simple past

📕 Topic 7: Past Perfect — Earlier Past, Sequencing, Causes Before Results ▼

When & Why to Use Past Perfect

The Past Perfect describes an action completed BEFORE another past action. It creates clear sequencing in narratives and shows cause-effect relationships in the past. For IELTS:

  • Speaking Part 2: Adding depth — "I had never seen snow before I moved to Canada"
  • Writing Task 1: Sequencing changes — "By 2010, the population had already exceeded 10 million"
  • Writing Task 1 Maps: Showing what existed before changes — "The area had been farmland before…"
  • Writing Task 2: Historical background — "Before the internet was invented, people had relied on…"
  • Reading: Understanding narrative sequence and cause-effect chains

Structure & Form

Affirmative: Subject + had + V3 (past participle)
Negative: Subject + had + not + V3
Question: Had + Subject + V3?

Key patterns:
• By + past time, Subject + had + V3
• Before + simple past, Subject + had + V3
• After + Subject + had + V3, + simple past
• Subject + had already + V3 + when/before + simple past

Signal Words / Time Expressions

beforeafterby the timealreadyuntil thenpreviouslyonceby 2010prior tonever…before

📗 Topic 8: Future Forms — will, going to, present continuous, academic predictions ▼

When & Why to Use Future Forms

English has multiple ways to express the future, each with distinct meaning. For IELTS, choosing the right future form shows sophisticated grammatical control:

  • will: Spontaneous decisions, predictions based on opinion, promises, certainty about the future
  • be going to: Pre-planned intentions, predictions based on evidence
  • Present Continuous: Fixed arrangements with specific time
  • is likely to / is expected to / is projected to / is predicted to: Academic hedged predictions (essential for Band 7+ Writing)
  • Future Perfect (will have + V3): Completion before a future deadline
  • Future Continuous (will be + V-ing): Action in progress at a future time

📒 Topic 9: Tense Consistency — Avoiding Shifts, Maintaining Logical Time ▼

When & Why Tense Consistency Matters

Tense consistency means maintaining the same time reference within a paragraph or section unless there is a clear reason to shift. Unnecessary tense shifts confuse the reader and lower your band score. For IELTS:

  • Writing Task 1: Don't mix past and present when describing data from one time period
  • Writing Task 2: Keep each paragraph in a consistent time frame
  • Speaking: Maintain tense when telling a story; don't jump between past and present randomly
  • Reading: Recognizing logical time shifts helps with True/False/Not Given and matching questions

Rules for Tense Consistency

Rule 1: Within a paragraph, maintain the base tense

✗ The population increased from 5 to 8 million. The graph shows that it reaches a peak in 2015. By 2020, it will have grown further.
✓ The population increased from 5 to 8 million. It then reached a peak in 2015 before declining slightly by 2020. (consistent simple past throughout)

Rule 2: Tense shifts must be motivated by a change in time

✓ "In the past, people relied on traditional media. However, in recent years, social media has become the primary source of news." (shift from past → present perfect is motivated by "in the past" → "in recent years")

Rule 3: In narratives (Speaking Part 2), use past tenses consistently

✗ "I went to Japan last year. The food is amazing. We visit many temples and I take lots of photos."
✓ "I went to Japan last year. The food was amazing. We visited many temples and I took lots of photos."

Rule 4: General statements stay in present simple, even within past narratives

✓ "I visited a solar farm last year. Solar energy is one of the cleanest forms of power." (general truth stays in present simple — this shift IS logical)

💡 Legitimate tense shifts always have a reason:

📓 Topic 10: Mixed Tense Control — Choosing the Best Tense, Recognizing Distractors ▼

When & Why Mixed Tense Control Matters

Mixed Tense Control is the ability to choose the best tense based on context and explain why one tense is better than another. This is the highest-level grammar skill tested in IELTS:

  • Band 7+ candidates can switch between tenses purposefully within a paragraph
  • They can identify distractor options that seem correct but aren't
  • They understand why one tense is better than another in specific contexts
  • They control time references across sentences, paragraphs, and entire essays

Decision Framework: How to Choose the Right Tense

Step 1: Identify the time reference
Is it past (finished), present (current), or future (not yet happened)? Look for signal words.
Step 2: Determine the aspect
Is it a fact/completed action (simple), in progress (continuous), connecting two times (perfect), or ongoing duration (perfect continuous)?
Step 3: Check for special contexts
Is it a graph overview (always present simple)? A conditional (special rules)? A stative verb (no continuous)?
Step 4: Verify consistency
Does this tense match the time reference of the surrounding sentences? Is a shift justified?

10 IELTS Examples — Why One Tense Wins

1. "The graph ______ changes in population." → shows (Simple Present)
Why not "is showing"? A graph permanently shows data — it's not a temporary action. "Is showing" implies the graph might show something different later, which is illogical.
2. "Sales ______ significantly between 2005 and 2010." → increased (Simple Past)
Why not "have increased"? The time period (2005–2010) is completed and specific. Present perfect needs an open-ended or unspecified time.
3. "Technology ______ education dramatically." (discussing current relevance) → has transformed (Present Perfect)
Why not "transformed"? Without a specific past time, we emphasise that the transformation is relevant NOW. Present perfect makes this connection.
4. "While I ______ for the bus, it started raining." → was waiting (Past Continuous)
Why not "waited"? "While" signals an ongoing background action. "Waited" would imply a completed single action, losing the simultaneous-event meaning.
5. "By 2030, renewable energy ______ fossil fuels." → will have replaced (Future Perfect)
Why not "will replace"? "By 2030" signals completion before a deadline. Future perfect is the correct form for "completed by" deadlines.
6. "I ______ English for eight years." (still studying) → have been studying (Present Perfect Continuous)
Why not "study"? The duration from past to present requires present perfect. The continuous form emphasises the ongoing nature.
7. "Before the crisis ______, experts ______ about the risks." → hit / had warned (Simple Past / Past Perfect)
Why past perfect for "warned"? The warning happened BEFORE the crisis. Past perfect shows this earlier time relationship.
8. "If governments ______ more, the situation ______ worse." → don't act / will get (First Conditional)
Why not "won't act"? In conditional sentences, the if-clause uses simple present, not "will". "Will" goes only in the result clause.
9. "The cost of living ______ rapidly these days." → is rising (Present Continuous)
Why not "rises"? "These days" signals a current, temporary trend. Simple present would suggest a permanent, unchanging fact.
10. "She ______ here since she was born." → has lived (Present Perfect)
Why not "lives"? "Since" requires present perfect to show duration from a past point to now. "Lives" doesn't convey this connection.

Quick Decision Tree: Which Tense?

Is the action in the PAST?
  • Specific finished time: Simple Past
  • Background/interrupted: Past Continuous
  • Before another past event: Past Perfect
Is it in the PRESENT?
  • General fact/routine: Simple Present
  • Current trend/temporary: Present Continuous
  • Past→now connection: Present Perfect
  • Ongoing duration: Present Perfect Continuous
Is it in the FUTURE?
  • Prediction/opinion: will + V1
  • Academic prediction: is likely/expected to
  • Completion deadline: Future Perfect
  • Fixed arrangement: Present Continuous

Top 10 Rules for Band 7+ Tense Control

  1. Task 1 overview = ALWAYS Simple Present ("The graph shows…")
  2. "Since" = ALWAYS Present Perfect ("has increased since 2010")
  3. Specific past time = ALWAYS Simple Past ("increased in 2010")
  4. "By" + future deadline = Future Perfect ("will have completed by 2030")
  5. "While" = Past Continuous for background ("while I was studying…")
  6. If-clause = NEVER "will" ("if I study" NOT "if I will study")
  7. Stative verbs = NEVER continuous ("I know" NOT "I am knowing")
  8. "For + duration" to now = Present Perfect/Continuous ("for 5 years")
  9. Tense shifts need motivation — never shift randomly within a paragraph
  10. Mix 4-6 tenses in Task 2 — variety shows range, the key Band 7 criterion
